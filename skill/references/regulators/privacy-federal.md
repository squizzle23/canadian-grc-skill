# PIPEDA — Curated Reference (Federal Private-Sector Privacy)

Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (S.C. 2000, c. 5). Canada's federal private-sector privacy law, administered by the Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada (OPC).

**Currency:** The `P-8.6.pdf` file is the consolidated Act current to March 17, 2026, last amended March 4, 2025.

## Application — who PIPEDA covers

PIPEDA applies to organizations engaged in **commercial activity**, except where provinces have **substantially similar** legislation. In those cases, the provincial law applies intra-province and PIPEDA applies to:
- Inter-provincial or international commercial activity
- Federal works, undertakings, and businesses (banks, telecoms, airlines, federally regulated transport, broadcasters)

**Provinces with substantially similar private-sector laws:**
- **Quebec** — Act Respecting the Protection of Personal Information in the Private Sector (Loi 25, chapter P-39.1)
- **British Columbia** — Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA BC)
- **Alberta** — Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA AB)

**All other provinces** (Ontario except PHIPA-scope health data, Saskatchewan, Manitoba, New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, PEI, Newfoundland, territories): PIPEDA applies to private-sector commercial activity.

**Health-sector overlay:** Provincial health privacy laws (e.g., PHIPA in Ontario) apply to "health information custodians." PIPEDA applies to health information collected by non-custodian organizations for commercial purposes.

## The Ten Fair Information Principles (Schedule 1)

PIPEDA is built on the CSA Model Code, codified in Schedule 1. These ten principles (sections 4.1–4.10 of Schedule 1) are the substantive rules.

| # | Principle | Reference | Summary |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Accountability | 4.1 | Designated individual accountable for compliance; policies and practices in place |
| 2 | Identifying Purposes | 4.2 | Purposes for collection identified at or before collection |
| 3 | Consent | 4.3 | Knowledge and consent of individual required for collection, use, disclosure |
| 4 | Limiting Collection | 4.4 | Limited to what's necessary for identified purposes; fair and lawful means |
| 5 | Limiting Use, Disclosure, and Retention | 4.5 | Used/disclosed only for purposes collected (with consent or law); retained only as long as necessary |
| 6 | Accuracy | 4.6 | Accurate, complete, up-to-date as necessary for purposes |
| 7 | Safeguards | 4.7 | Security safeguards appropriate to sensitivity of the information |
| 8 | Openness | 4.8 | Policies and practices readily available |
| 9 | Individual Access | 4.9 | Individual access on request; ability to challenge accuracy |
| 10 | Challenging Compliance | 4.10 | Process to address complaints and challenges |

**Important drafting note:** The Schedule uses "should" in many places (CSA Model Code language) while the Act uses "shall." Sections 5(1)–(3) of the Act turn "should" into legally binding obligations for most Schedule 1 provisions. Distinguish between Schedule 1 language and how section 5 makes it enforceable.

## Division 1.1 — Breaches of Security Safeguards (sections 10.1–10.3)

This is the enforceable breach regime. These sections were added by 2015 amendments and are the primary PIPEDA incident-response citations.

### Section 10.1 — Report to the Commissioner

**10.1(1)** An organization **shall** report to the Commissioner any breach of security safeguards involving personal information under its control if **it is reasonable in the circumstances to believe that the breach creates a real risk of significant harm to an individual**.

**10.1(2)** The report shall contain prescribed information and be made in the prescribed form and manner **as soon as feasible** after the organization determines that the breach has occurred.

**10.1(3)** Unless otherwise prohibited by law, an organization shall **notify an individual** of any breach involving their personal information if the same real-risk-of-significant-harm test is met.

**10.1(4)** Notification contents: sufficient to allow the individual to understand the significance of the breach and take steps to reduce or mitigate harm; plus prescribed information.

**10.1(5)** Notification shall be conspicuous and given directly to the individual in the prescribed form and manner, unless prescribed circumstances permit indirect notification.

**10.1(6)** Notification shall be given **as soon as feasible** after the organization determines the breach has occurred.

**10.1(7) — Definition of "significant harm":**
> Significant harm includes bodily harm, humiliation, damage to reputation or relationships, loss of employment, business or professional opportunities, financial loss, identity theft, negative effects on the credit record and damage to or loss of property.

**10.1(8) — Relevant factors for "real risk of significant harm":**
- (a) the sensitivity of the personal information involved in the breach;
- (b) the probability that the personal information has been, is being, or will be misused; and
- (c) any other prescribed factor.

### Section 10.2 — Notification to Other Organizations

**10.2(1)** An organization that notifies an individual under 10.1(3) shall notify any other organization, government institution, or part thereof, of the breach if the notifying organization believes the other can reduce the risk of harm or mitigate it, or if prescribed conditions are satisfied.

**10.2(2)** Notification shall be given **as soon as feasible** after the organization determines the breach has occurred.

**10.2(3)(a)–(b)** Permits disclosure of personal information to the other organization without the individual's knowledge or consent, solely for reducing or mitigating risk of harm.

### Section 10.3 — Records

**10.3(1)** An organization **shall** keep and maintain a record of **every** breach of security safeguards involving personal information under its control — not just reportable breaches.

**10.3(2)** The Commissioner may on request require access to or a copy of the record.

**Prescribed retention (from regulations):** 24 months from the day the organization determines the breach has occurred.

### The "real risk of significant harm" test — practical application

- **Sensitivity:** Financial, health, identification, biometric, and authentication data are high-sensitivity. Employment records, contact information at the other end.
- **Probability of misuse:** Breach method matters — targeted attack by a threat actor with a known motive is higher probability than a lost unencrypted laptop.
- **Combined assessment:** High sensitivity + low probability may still trigger notification if consequences are severe (e.g., medical records in a ransomware dump).

## Other Division 1 provisions worth citing

- **Section 2 — Definitions:** "personal information" is defined broadly as information about an identifiable individual. Includes opinion, inferences, and identifiers.
- **Section 4 — Application:** sets the commercial-activity trigger and the exclusion for substantially-similar provincial law.
- **Section 5 — Compliance with Schedule 1:** the provision that makes Schedule 1 legally binding.
- **Section 7 — Collection, Use, and Disclosure Without Knowledge or Consent:** lists the specific circumstances where consent is not required (journalism, public interest, etc.).
- **Section 11 — Individual Complaints:** complaint process to the Commissioner.

## Output patterns for PIPEDA questions

**When asked about breach reporting:**
1. Cite **section 10.1(1)** for the OPC report obligation, **10.1(3)** for individual notification, **10.2(1)** for other-organization notification.
2. State the trigger: "real risk of significant harm" per **10.1(1)** and **10.1(3)**, with factors in **10.1(8)**.
3. State the timeline: "as soon as feasible" per **10.1(2), 10.1(6), 10.2(2)** — PIPEDA does not specify an hour-count, unlike OSFI (24hr) or CIRO (3 days).
4. State the register obligation: **10.3(1)** covers **every** breach (not just reportable), retained 24 months per regulations.
5. If the organization is also regulated: map the sector regulator's clock separately (OSFI 24hr, FSRA 72hr, CIRO 3 days, AMF 24hr).

**When asked whether PIPEDA or a provincial law applies:**
1. Determine: (a) commercial activity? (b) which province(s) involved? (c) health information and health custodian?
2. Apply the substantially-similar-law exclusion: Quebec → Loi 25; BC → PIPA BC; Alberta → PIPA AB intra-province.
3. Health custodians in Ontario → PHIPA for health information; PIPEDA still applies to commercial-activity personal information outside the custodian scope.
4. Federal works → always PIPEDA (banks, telecoms, airlines).

**When asked to write a privacy notice or policy:**
1. Cover all ten Schedule 1 principles.
2. Reference section 10.1 for breach notification commitments.
3. If the organization touches Quebec residents, layer Loi 25 requirements (PIA, consent, cross-border assessment, CAI notification).
4. If the organization touches EU/UK residents, GDPR/UK GDPR is a parallel regime with stricter rules; note the overlap.

**When asked about cross-border data:**
1. PIPEDA does not prohibit cross-border transfers — but section 4.1 Schedule 1 (Accountability principle) requires an organization to remain accountable for personal information transferred for processing.
2. Contractual or other means must provide comparable protection.
3. Quebec Loi 25 adds a specific cross-border assessment requirement (not PIPEDA).

**Output grade (for compliance advice):**
- 🔴 Critical Gap — missing breach notification (10.1), no breach register (10.3), or violation of Schedule 1 safeguards principle (4.7) in circumstances involving sensitive data.
- 🟡 Warning — breach register incomplete, privacy notice missing required Schedule 1 elements, inadequate accountability designation (4.1).
- 🟢 Compliant — all ten principles addressed, breach response documented, register maintained, accountable individual designated.

**Sales context openers:**
- "PIPEDA section 10.3 requires a register of every breach — not just the reportable ones. And section 10.1 is 'as soon as feasible,' which the OPC has interpreted tightly. What's your current breach-record capability?"
- "Schedule 1 Principle 7 — Safeguards — requires protection appropriate to sensitivity. Continuous visibility into your external attack surface is the defensible posture. Are you demonstrating that continuously to your board?"
